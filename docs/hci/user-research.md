# User Research

## Project
MovieSphere is a movie recommendation web app for students and casual viewers who often know the kind of mood they want, but do not know the exact movie title to search for.

## Research Goal
Understand how users currently discover movies, where friction happens, and what kind of recommendation support feels helpful rather than overwhelming.

## Methods
- Semi-structured interviews with 4 target users
- Lightweight competitor scan of Netflix, Letterboxd, and TMDB discovery flows
- Context-of-use notes focused on mobile and laptop evening browsing sessions

## Participant Summary
1. Engineering student, 21, watches 3-4 movies per month, usually asks friends for suggestions
2. Design student, 22, watches for mood and aesthetics, gets frustrated by long scrolling
3. Working professional, 25, wants quick suggestions after work with minimal decision effort
4. Casual viewer, 20, remembers actors or vibes more often than titles or genres

## Key Findings
1. Users often start with vague intent such as "something emotional" or "something mind-bending".
2. Existing platforms overload users with rows, thumbnails, and weak explanations for why a movie is recommended.
3. People trust recommendations more when they can either set a mood or start from a movie they already like.
4. Short metadata matters. Users want year, rating, and a compact overview before deciding.
5. Search should feel forgiving. Participants frequently remembered partial titles only.

## Personas

### Persona 1: Aarav, the undecided explorer
- Goal: Find a good movie fast without scrolling through dozens of options
- Pain point: Too many choices make him postpone watching anything
- Need: A guided flow that narrows choices by mood and genre

### Persona 2: Riya, the taste-driven viewer
- Goal: Find movies similar to favorites she already loves
- Pain point: Generic "similar titles" sections often feel random
- Need: A clean way to seed recommendations from one known movie

## Design Implications
1. Support both open-ended discovery and seed-based recommendation.
2. Keep controls lightweight and highly scannable.
3. Use strong visual hierarchy so users can compare options quickly.
4. Surface a small, curated recommendation set rather than an endless wall of content.
